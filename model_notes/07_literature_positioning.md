# Module 7: Literature Positioning

## 1. Purpose

This module records how the paper should be positioned after the clean-axis
revision. It is a positioning guide, not a verified bibliography. Exact
citations must be checked before paper drafting.

The literature role is now provenance discipline, not broad name-checking. Each
baseline primitive must be traceable to the MASTER provenance table.

---

## 2. JMP positioning [Accepted baseline]

The project should be positioned as a macro-industrial policy / quantitative
macro-development paper about dynamic capacity misallocation. It studies how
decentralized local industrial policy affects durable and partly irreversible
NEV capacity investment, demand absorption, sectoral capital returns, and
aggregate welfare relative to implementable centralized coordination. It is not
a pure empirical IO paper, a BLP demand-estimation paper, a supplier innovation
paper, a corporate-finance paper, or a standard DSGE/RBC business-cycle paper.

The core object is a solvable multi-region macro model with:

- decentralized local industrial policy;
- local expansionary policy platforms that move investment and installed
  capacity;
- private firm and project investment responses;
- durable and partly irreversible NEV installed capacity;
- demand absorption and price/utilization adjustment;
- NEV and outside-sector capital allocation;
- consumer-surplus, producer-surplus, and fiscal-cost accounting;
- MPK/MRPK gaps and net welfare counterfactuals;
- a constrained centralized policy benchmark.

The paper should not describe the life cycle as an exogenous split between
predefined stages. It should describe the dynamic path generated by policy
platforms, capacity investment, durable installed capacity, demand absorption,
price adjustment, utilization, profits, and capital returns. Entry, selection,
learning, and innovation remain mechanism or extension objects unless the model
explicitly promotes them with provenance.

## 5. Positioning statement [Accepted baseline]

The paper contributes a macro model of decentralized local industrial policy in
which local governments play a Markov-Nash policy game, firms actively respond
through capacity investment and NEV installed capacity, realized demand is
disciplined by aggregate demand absorption, and the central comparison is
between decentralized equilibrium and an implementable constrained planner that
coordinates policy instruments.

The paper is not a generic argument against industrial policy. Existing studies
explain how China's NEV industry rose. This paper asks how the marginal welfare
return to the same decentralized local implementation changes as durable
capacity, demand absorption, prices, utilization, profits, and MPK/MRPK gaps
evolve. The key return-divergence condition is
$\zeta^{L,NEV}(Z_t)>0$ but $\zeta^{C,NEV}(Z_t)\leq0$: local expansion remains
rational locally while the aggregate return to the same policy has fallen or
turned negative. It is also not an argument that lower prices are mechanically
harmful: low prices can raise consumer or final-good welfare, and the paper's
welfare comparison against $CP$ can be negative, near zero, or positive.

## 6. Literature-use rules

1. Use Chen-Liu-Song for local versus central intervention indexes, the
   local-central policy gap, policy platforms, and fiscal-autonomy heterogeneity.
   Do not use it to claim NEV life-cycle dynamics, capacity laws, or price wars.
2. Use Aggregate Demand and Irreversible Investment for durable and partly
   irreversible capital, demand absorption, and MRPK-dispersion logic. Do not
   import working-capital financial frictions, endogenous default risk, banking
   pricing, hand-to-mouth households, or full heterogeneous-firm recursive
   equilibrium into the baseline.
3. Use Barwick-Kalouptsidi-Zahur for policy effects on entry, investment,
   capacity, idle capacity, and welfare counterfactual discipline. Do not use it
   to derive decentralized local-government competition.
4. Use Kalouptsidi for capacity accumulation, time-to-build or adjustment
   friction, demand absorption, and price response. Do not import shipping
   resale markets, scrappage, fleet-age structure, or full dynamic IO estimation
   into the baseline.
5. Use Klepper only for industry-life-cycle interpretation: entry, exit,
   innovation, and market structure over maturity. Do not use it as a policy or
   welfare model.
6. Use Hsieh-Klenow and related macro misallocation papers for MPK/MRPK gaps,
   TFPR dispersion, and aggregate output or TFP-loss language.
7. Keep SOE, export, soft-budget, production-network, supply-chain finance,
   supplier innovation, BLP, and differentiated-demand papers in extension notes
   unless a later decision promotes them.
